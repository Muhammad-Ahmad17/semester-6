p014:00333:   It would be a shame to bring an entire writing project to a halt just because
p014:00334:you didn’t know how to spell gyroscope or schnapps. So get a dictionary and
p014:00335:keep it in the room where you write, no more than an arm’s length away. In
p014:00336:fact, get two. Get a hardcover for its comprehensiveness and a paperback for
p014:00337:convenience. If you prefer your reference books in digital format, you can get
p014:00338:an e-book version or use an online app.
p014:00339:   Also, get an encyclopedia. If you can’t afford a big set, get a single-volume
p014:00340:encyclopedia. If you can’t afford a subscription to Encyclopædia Britannica
p014:00341:or the like online, there are free online encyclopedias such as
p014:00342:encyclopedia.com. You can use Wikipedia as a resource for background
p014:00343:information or to point you to other sources, but it should not be cited as a
p014:00344:source. It is created and edited by volunteers.
p014:00345:   And get a thesaurus. Thesaurus means “treasury”; the thesaurus you buy
p014:00346:will be a treasury of synonyms, words that are close in meaning to the one
p014:00347:you want. It is a book that will lead you to that perfect word you know is
p014:00348:loitering on the outskirts of your brain.
p014:00349:   Roget’s Thesaurus is arranged in two sections. The first section contains
p014:00350:hundreds of clusters of related words and phrases. The second section is an
p014:00351:index listing all the words in the first section alphabetically and telling you
p014:00352:where they appear in that section.
p014:00353:   Let’s say, for example, that in a letter you want to assure the owner of the
p014:00354:company you work for that you will most certainly try to recover the four
p014:00355:billion dollars you lost on the papier-mâché deal, but recover isn’t quite the
p014:00356:word you want to use, and you’re not sure what is. So you whip out your
p014:00357:pocket edition of Roget’s Thesaurus, turn to the index, and look up recover.
p014:00358:There you’ll find the numbers 660, 775, and 790. You turn to cluster 660 and
p014:00359:you find recover along with its cousins rally, revive, pull through, reappear,
p014:00360:and others. If you don’t like anything you find there, you turn to the other
p014:00361:numbers, and the thesaurus will lead you to redeem, get back, salvage, and so
p014:00362:on.
p014:00363:   You can find thesauruses in paperback and hardcover, as well as online, and
p014:00364:Roget’s is not the only one. I do not recommend the ones that are arranged
p015:00366:as useful.
p015:00367:   After you have acquired a dictionary, an encyclopedia, and a thesaurus, you
p015:00368:can acquire other reference books as time, taste, and money allow. Their
p015:00369:importance depends largely on what sort of writing you do and how much.
p015:00370:   Here are a few reference books you might find useful.
p015:00371:   Finding Facts Fast by Alden Todd is a good reference book for any writer
p015:00372:who has to do research.
p015:00373:   The World Almanac and Book of Facts will tell you how many tomatoes
p015:00374:were grown in New Jersey last year and a good many other things you might
p015:00375:not find anywhere else.
p015:00376:   The Book of Lists by David Wallechinsky, Irving Wallace, and Amy
p015:00377:Wallace is fun to read and rich with useful information.
p015:00378:   An effective online resource is refseek.com, which is a web search engine
p015:00379:that acts as a directory of encyclopedias, directories, almanacs, and other
p015:00380:reference resources.
p015:00381:   The Careful Writer by Theodore M. Bernstein is an excellent grammar
p015:00382:guide.

p015:00386:2. Expand Your Vocabulary
p015:00387:
p015:00388:   Everybody has heard tips for improving vocabulary. Learn a new word in
p015:00389:the morning and use it three times before sunset and it’s yours, etc. There are
p015:00390:many books that will help you stretch your vocabulary. The best-known one is
p015:00391:Thirty Days to a More Powerful Vocabulary by Wilfred Funk and Norman
p015:00392:Lewis. Read that book or one like it.
p015:00393:   There are websites such as vocabulary.com and lingualift .com if you prefer
p015:00394:to work with your vocabulary online. For those on the go, apps such as
p015:00395:Vocabulary Builder by Magoosh and Vocab24 by WiFi Study are free and
p015:00396:highly rated for improving your vocabulary.
p015:00397:   But the most important vocabulary for the writer is not the one that will
p015:00398:take in uxorious tomorrow and soubrette the next day. It’s the one he or she
p015:00399:already has. For the writer of average intelligence and education, learning
p015:00400:new words is much less important than learning to use easily the words he or
p015:00401:she already knows.
p015:00402:   Think for a minute. How many synonyms can you come up with for the
p015:00403:noun plan?
p016:00405:blueprint. If you concentrated for a minute, you might have come up with ten
p016:00406:words that you already knew. But how many of them would have come easily
p016:00407:to mind while you were writing a letter to the boss about your potentially
p016:00408:lucrative new . . . uh . . . plan?
p016:00409:   The only way to make your vocabulary more accessible is to use it. If you
p016:00410:want all those short but interesting words waiting at the front of your brain
p016:00411:when you need them, you must move them to the front of your brain before
p016:00412:you need them.
p016:00413:   Stop to think about other word possibilities when you write, and eventually
p016:00414:they will come so quickly that you won’t have to stop.
p016:00415:   Pause before you speak. Then insert some of those good but neglected
p016:00416:words.
p016:00417:   And when you drive home from work at night, pick out an object along the
p016:00418:road and see how many synonyms you can think of before you pass it. There’s
p016:00419:a house over there. But it’s also a dwelling, an abode, a building, a bungalow,
p016:00420:perhaps, or maybe a cottage. It’s a home for somebody, it’s headquarters for a
p016:00421:family, and it’s a shelter and a structure, too.

p016:00425:3. Improve Your Spelling
p016:00426:
p016:00427:   There aren’t many firm rules that apply to the spelling of English words.
p016:00428:Mostly, good spelling is a matter of forming the right mental associations and
p016:00429:developing an eye for words that look a little weird.
p016:00430:   In the dictionary, look up any word that you’re not sure of. If you have
p016:00431:been misspelling it, write it correctly ten times. Invent a visual image for the
p016:00432:correct spelling. For example: The Sahara desert only has one s, like Sahara,
p016:00433:but the dessert after a meal has a second s, like a second helping.
p016:00434:   How will better spelling improve your writing? Well, for one thing, you
p016:00435:won’t write desert when you mean dessert. More important, it will improve
p016:00436:your writing by reducing the number of times you annoy the reader. A few
p016:00437:misspelled words will jar the reader’s concentration, and a lot of misspelled
p016:00438:words will wreck your credibility. Right or wrong, the reader will perceive
p016:00439:you as . . . well, stupid, to put it bluntly. If you don’t have the respect of the
p016:00440:reader, your writing will not work.
p016:00441:   Spell check is available in most word processing programs; however, it
p016:00442:does have its limitations. So although it catches many mistakes that are
p017:00444:others as incorrect when they are not. If you spell a word correctly, spell
p017:00445:check will sometimes let it go, even if the usage is incorrect. Examples are
p017:00446:principle vs. principal, there vs. their vs. they’re, and dear vs. deer. You need
p017:00447:to know how the word should be spelled so you can check the spell checker.

p018:00503:4. Read
p018:00504:
p018:00505:   If you are an architect, you should certainly read architectural literature. If
p018:00506:you are in computers, you must keep up with what’s being written about
p018:00507:terabytes, hacking, and the latest operating systems. Reading the books and
p018:00508:trade magazines as well as checking out the key websites of your particular
p018:00509:field will not only keep you informed; it will show you how experienced
p018:00510:writers are turning the jargon and the complexities of your vocation into
p018:00511:readable prose.
p018:00512:   But no matter what your field of expertise, you should also read books,
p018:00513:magazines, and newspapers designed for the general reader.
p018:00514:   Though the daily paper and online news sites contain much that is swill,
p018:00515:they also contain some good writing. From them you can learn to write leanly,
p018:00516:to get to the point, and to compress several facts into a single clear sentence.
p018:00517:   If you read mysteries and romances, you will discover how writers create
p018:00518:curiosity and build tension. You will also learn how to construct an event, a
p018:00519:person, or a place with just a few well-chosen words.
p018:00520:   Read novels. You will see how words can be used to communicate
p018:00521:subtleties and stir emotions, how words can be arranged one way to make you
p019:00523:   Read magazine articles and you will see how quotes are pared down from
p019:00524:lengthy interviews until they contain nothing but the words that matter. Notice
p019:00525:how opinions are supported by facts. Watch to see how the writer makes his
p019:00526:or her points by calling on outside help such as scientific reports, quotes from
p019:00527:books, surveys, etc. Then go to the online version and see whether they have a
p019:00528:longer version so you can see what was edited for the print copy.
p019:00529:   Read. And listen to what you read. Listen for the sound of the language, the
p019:00530:music. Note the punctuation, the spelling, the logical progression of
p019:00531:information. And find the things that fail, also. Listen to how two similar
p019:00532:sounds close together can cause a disturbing noise in your head. Hear how the
p019:00533:use of the wrong word wakes you from your reading spell. Be a critical reader
p019:00534:and look upon all that you read as a lesson in good writing.

p019:00539:5. Take a Class
p019:00540:
p019:00541:   If you don’t believe that good writing can be taught, you shouldn’t be
p019:00542:reading this book. If you do believe good writing can be taught, you could
p019:00543:benefit from a class.
p019:00544:   You don’t have to sign up for a three-credit course at the local university.
p019:00545:You can find a creative writing or English composition course in most adult
p019:00546:education and extension programs or online.
p019:00547:   There are specific courses designed for particular types of writers. For
p019:00548:example, there are business writing courses that thoroughly cover the formal
p019:00549:English required in business correspondence. A course in nonfiction writing
p019:00550:will provide you with some research techniques that you wouldn’t get
p019:00551:elsewhere. And a course in writing for television would be invaluable if that’s
p019:00552:your interest, as there are many rules of form a scriptwriter must follow.
p019:00553:   Generally, a writing course is as good or as bad as the teacher. Good
p019:00554:teachers and bad teachers are found at all levels, so ask around.
p019:00555:   Whether it’s a course at the local high school or a course at Harvard, in my
p019:00556:opinion you should steer clear of any teacher who speaks with a British accent
p019:00557:but has never been to England and any teacher who insists you must read
p019:00558:Moby Dick before writing your first paper. Point yourself toward the eager,
p019:00559:unpretentious teacher who is actually publishing stories, articles, and books.
p019:00560:   “But why,” you ask, “should I take a writing class at all after reading a
p019:00561:wonderful book like this?”
p020:00563:  1. With a teacher and other students reading your work, you will be better
p020:00564:able to learn what your particular faults and virtues are.
p020:00565:  2. Knowing that the class or teacher is going to read your work, you will
p020:00566:work harder at making your writing good.
p020:00567:  3. Your own writing mistakes are often invisible to you, but they will
p020:00568:become obvious when you see them in the work of fellow students.

p021:00615:

p021:00623:    the canvassing committee? Is it the president of the company? Is it the

p021:00628:    ask somebody else.

p021:00635:    restrooms. Although most of these details are accessible online, they are
p021:00636:    not always updated in a timely manner. Sometimes it’s best to find out
p021:00637:    for yourself.

p021:00639:    emergency, ask somebody. Like a waitress at the Old Timer’s, or the

p022:00643:     to find it, ask the librarian. He or she will find it or direct you to the

p022:00655:8. Write in Your Head
p022:00656:
p022:00657:   When I was a reporter for a local newspaper, I used to leave a school
p022:00658:committee or municipal meeting around eleven p.m. in Hudson and drive
p022:00659:eight miles to the newspaper office in Marlboro, where I would write my
p022:00660:stories for the next day’s edition.
p022:00661:   Often I arrived after other reporters. But almost invariably I would write
p022:00662:my stories, hand them in, and drive home before the others. I was able to do
p022:00663:this, not because I am a faster typist, but because I started writing before I got
p022:00664:to the office. I wrote the first draft in my head during the drive to Marlboro. In
p022:00665:my mind I planned the lead, decided what information I could ignore, and
p022:00666:organized my material. By the time I reached the office, I knew what I wanted
p022:00667:to write, and when I sat down at the typewriter, it was like pushing the “play”
p022:00668:button on a tape recorder. Everything I had recorded in my brain came out.
p022:00669:   So if you have a writing job, write in your head. Clear up the
p022:00670:inconsistencies while you’re brushing your teeth. Get your thoughts organized
p022:00671:while you’re driving to work. Think of a slant during lunch. And most
p022:00672:important, come up with a beginning, a lead, so that you won’t end up staring
p022:00673:at your keyboard as if it had just arrived from another galaxy. If you have
p022:00674:spent time writing in your head, you’ll have a head start. The writing will
p022:00675:come easier, and you’ll finish sooner.
p023:00677:
p023:00678:   For most writers the hardest part of any writing project is getting started. I
p023:00679:often begin by staring at the blank page as if it is some vile substance that has
p023:00680:been spilled on my desk. Then, no matter how alert I was when I arrived at
p023:00681:the keyboard, I become almost terminally drowsy. My eyes droop. My
p023:00682:shoulders sag. Finally, I begin to think, “Well, maybe I should take a little nap
p023:00683:first; then I’ll be well rested for writing.” Usually my puritan conscience
p023:00684:cancels that plan. So I take on the expression of a man who has just been
p023:00685:strapped into a dentist’s chair and begin to write. As soon as I have words on
p023:00686:paper, agony departs. I love writing. It’s getting started that I abhor.
p023:00687:   I tell you this so that you won’t feel alone. You probably go through similar
p023:00688:hell before you write. Almost everybody does. The way to eliminate most of
p023:00689:these traumas is to write in large blocks of time rather than to try to write for
p023:00690:ten minutes here and there. Look at your schedule. When will you be left
p023:00691:undisturbed for an hour or two? Can you lock the door? Turn off the phone?
p023:00692:You will get more writing done in an undisturbed hour than you would in a
p023:00693:dozen ten-minute spurts.
p023:00694:   It is also important to find a quiet place to write. Few people can write their
p023:00695:best when the phone is ringing and the kids are clamoring for whatever it is
p023:00696:that makes kids clamor. A den in a noisy house would probably produce less
p023:00697:writing than the backseat of a car in a quiet garage. So find someplace quiet.
p023:00698:Is there a day when everybody else is out of the house? Does a friend have a
p023:00699:cottage? Does your company have an empty office?
p023:00700:   If you can’t find a quiet place to write, use earplugs.

p025:00748:
p025:00749:  There is no one right way to keep a journal. But if you have some sort of
p025:00750:notebook or diary that you return to often with your written thoughts,
p026:00752:to exercise your writing muscles.

p026:00768:3. Talk About What You’re Writing
p026:00769:
p026:00770:   When you’re looking for a job, you tell as many people as you can. There’s
p026:00771:always the chance that one of your friends knows about a suitable job

p026:00781:friend Karen read a book last week that had a chapter on your subject, your
p026:00782:cousin Louie might send you an appropriate newspaper clipping, somebody
p026:00783:else might remember a fitting quote from George Bernard Shaw, and your
p026:00784:brother James might remember something significant that he heard when he

p027:00806:
p027:00807:  Just as you need to get your body warmed up to run, you need a little

p027:00818:6. Organize Your Material
p027:00819:
p027:00820:   Certainly there is such a thing as being overorganized. Some writers
p027:00821:organize their material so thoroughly that everything they write comes out
p027:00822:looking like a hardware catalog. Your outline, whatever its form, should
p027:00823:contain enough slack for creativity and space for new thoughts on your
p027:00824:subject.
p027:00825:   But you should organize the material. Organizing will help lock in the logic
p027:00826:of what you say, and it will speed the writing process. Organizing will help to
p027:00827:create an overall unity in your story as well as several interior unities.
p027:00828:   There is no one right way to organize material for a story. Organization
p027:00829:depends on the nature of the work and, more importantly, on what works for
p028:00831:you a few tips:
p028:00832:
p028:00833:         Create a list of questions about your subject before you begin
p028:00834:    research, and keep related questions together. Go to many different
p028:00835:    sources for answers—even go to many sources for answers to a single
p028:00836:    question. Several answers to the same question are compelling when
p028:00837:    they are similar and fascinating when they are not.
p028:00838:         Gather much more material than you will use. Just as high water
p028:00839:    pressure makes more water flow faster, the greater weight of material
p028:00840:    you have gathered will make the words flow faster.
p028:00841:         As you create written material, whether you are photocopying at the
p028:00842:    library, transcribing taped interviews, or simply scribbling notes, write
p028:00843:    on one side of the paper only. That way you can slice up your material
p028:00844:    with a pair of scissors and rearrange it any way you want. While there
p028:00845:    are many computer programs to help writers organize and edit, you
p028:00846:    might be one who prefers to print out and work on hard copy.

p028:00850:7. Make a List
p028:00851:
p028:00852:   Some writers will not write a magazine article until they have constructed
p028:00853:an outline that is longer than the article they intend to write. Other writers
p028:00854:begin with no outline at all, though they probably have a vague outline in
p028:00855:mind.
p028:00856:   How long or detailed your outline is depends on the scope of what you
p028:00857:have to write and how secure you are with the material. But an outline is just
p028:00858:a list of elements you want to put into your writing, and for any story or
p028:00859:article you should make some sort of list, even if it’s just three words
p028:00860:scribbled on a scrap of paper. Write some key words for the issues you want
p028:00861:to cover, the facts you want to point to, the questions you want to pose.
p028:00862:Glance at the list as you work. This will help you decide what to write next.
p028:00863:   If, for example, you want to write a letter to your lawyer describing the
p028:00864:skullduggery your husband has been up to since the divorce agreement was
p028:00865:signed, you might scribble a list that looks like this:
p028:00866:
p028:00867:     1. Cheated me on the car payments.
p028:00868:     2. Sends support checks late.
p029:00870:
p029:00871:   Even if you are writing something short, such as a press release, it’s a good
p029:00872:idea to make a list of essential elements. Making a checklist: “Time. Date.
p029:00873:Place. Price.” Newspaper offices are always getting press releases that don’t
p029:00874:mention what time the pancake breakfast at the Boys’ Club begins.

p029:00879:8. Picture a Reader
p029:00880:
p029:00881:   Do you know who your reader is? Is your story going to be read by a
p029:00882:professor who knows everything but has very little time? Or is the reader a
p029:00883:layperson with no knowledge of your specialty? A little girl, perhaps? An
p029:00884:immigrant?
p029:00885:   Before you write, figure out whom you are trying to reach. Who is the
p029:00886:reader and what does he or she know?
p029:00887:   To write is not necessarily to communicate. Communication occurs in the
p029:00888:mind of the reader, and if that reader is not familiar with your terms and your
p029:00889:concepts, you might as well write them in Latvian. The computer terms that
p029:00890:are impressive in a letter to your software engineer will be gobbledygook in a
p029:00891:sales brochure aimed at people who have never used computers.
p029:00892:   Remember that when you write, the language you have to work with is not
p029:00893:your entire vocabulary, but only that portion of it that you share with the
p029:00894:reader. Just because you speak Portuguese doesn’t mean you should pepper
p029:00895:your story with Portuguese phrases. This reminder goes not just for words but
p029:00896:for historical allusions and the like. When you write, don’t think about how
p029:00897:smart you are; think about how smart your reader is. To do that you must
p029:00898:visualize him or her. Imagine your reader in the room with you. What is his
p029:00899:education? What are his attitudes? How important is this particular story to
p029:00900:him? Write as if you were in conversation with your readers. Listen to the
p029:00901:dialogue that would occur. Are your readers going to stop you and say, “Wait
p029:00902:a minute. Wait a minute. What’s a grumdocle?” If they are, then don’t use
p029:00903:grumdocle, or explain it when you do.
p029:00904:
p029:00905:
p029:00906:
p029:00907:9. Ask Yourself Why You Are Writing
p030:00909:Are you trying to make readers laugh? Are you trying to persuade them to
p030:00910:buy a product? Are you trying to advise them? To prove an argument? To
p030:00911:inform them so that they can make a decision?
p030:00912:   If you cannot answer the question “Why am I writing this?” then you
p030:00913:cannot wisely choose words, provide facts, include or exclude humor. You
p030:00914:must know what job you want done before you can pick the tools to do it.
p030:00915:And if you cannot state clearly at least one reason for writing your story,
p030:00916:article, or paper . . . don’t write it.

p032:00955:2. Write a Strong Lead
p032:00956:
p032:00957:   There is no precise definition of the lead. It can be the first sentence, the
p032:00958:first paragraph, even the first several paragraphs of your article or story. The
p032:00959:lead is whatever it takes to lead your readers so deeply into your story or
p032:00960:article that they will not turn back unless you stray from the path you have put
p032:00961:them on.
p032:00962:   Here are two leads that I have used:
p033:00964:   and stare down Essex Street all the way to the Hawthorne Boulevard. And,
p033:00965:   if you’re in luck, you might see something black coming around the corner,
p033:00966:   something black and bewildering, floating, like a hole in the sky growing
p033:00967:   larger as it comes toward you. For an instant it is as disturbing as a rustle
p033:00968:   in the night. Don’t be concerned. It is only Laurie Cabot.
p033:00969:
p033:00970:    John E. Rock kills people for their own good.
p033:00971:    “It’s all hypothetical, of course,” he says, waving a hand at his
p033:00972:   MacBook in the Framingham office of Rock Insurance.
p033:00973:
p033:00974:   Though the term “lead” is usually associated with nonfiction, the lead in
p033:00975:fiction is just as important. Here is how Gail Levine-Freidus began her novel
p033:00976:for children Popcorn:
p033:00977:
p033:00978:   You know how sometimes you suddenly get the feeling that someone is
p033:00979:   watching every move you make? The feeling sort of sneaks up on you and
p033:00980:   gives you the creeps, whatever they are. Well that’s exactly how I felt in Mr.
p033:00981:   Pettigrew’s English class just as I was starting to work on the last section
p033:00982:   of our test.
p033:00983:
p033:00984:   A lead should be provocative. It should have energy, excitement, an
p033:00985:implicit promise that something is going to happen or that some interesting
p033:00986:information will be revealed. It should create curiosity, get the reader asking
p033:00987:questions.
p033:00988:   The character of a good lead depends largely on the nature and length of
p033:00989:what you have written. A 500-word lead in an 800-word story is not a good
p033:00990:lead, but it could be a great lead in a 3,000-word story.
p033:00991:   Your lead should give readers something to care about before it gives them
p033:00992:dry background information. “Something to care about” usually means one of
p033:00993:two things. Either you give the readers information that affects them directly,
p033:00994:or you give them a human being with whom they can identify.
p033:00995:   Don’t begin a story in the company newsletter like this:
p033:00996:
p033:00997:   On March 27 the Board of Directors met at the Holiday Inn in Podunk. All
p033:00998:   but two members were present. John Burdick of the Tymecomp Agency
p033:00999:   presented the results of his time and productivity study. Mr. Burdick has
p033:01000:   spent six months in the four plants surveying daily production, employee
p033:01001:   attendance records, and overhead costs. He has spoken to employees and
p033:01002:   personnel managers. He described the effectiveness of a variety of work
p034:01004:   the company on a four-day workweek, effective June 1.
p034:01005:
p034:01006:  Employees would have to read the whole first paragraph before they found
p034:01007:what the story had to do with them. Most employees would not have
p034:01008:bothered.
p034:01009:  The provocative information, the information that will hook the reader and
p034:01010:compel him to keep reading, is at the end of the paragraph. It should be
p034:01011:moved to the front, and the lead should be:
p034:01012:
p034:01013:   On June 1 the company will go to a four-day workweek.
p034:01014:
p034:01015:  This next lead is the opening of my book about Las Vegas, High Stakes.
p034:01016:Instead of loading the opening with impersonal background material, I
p034:01017:brought my story immediately to life by giving the reader something to care
p034:01018:about. Note the two important elements in the lead: I made it visible—I
p034:01019:showed the reader something; and I made it human—I showed the reader
p034:01020:what the topic of the book meant to a real person: me.
p034:01021:
p034:01022:   In the spring of 1990 my friend Frank Strunk took me to Las Vegas. He
p034:01023:   lured me into a casino, my first time, his zillionth, and introduced me to the
p034:01024:   fine points of blackjack and craps. Even before my second $100 bill had
p034:01025:   been poked down into the darkness of the drop box under crap table
p034:01026:   number five at the Flamingo Hilton, I knew that I had found the missing
p034:01027:   piece in my life.
p034:01028:
p034:01029:   A lead like that will make your reader want to keep reading.
p034:01030:   One common mistake you should be aware of is the writing of two or three
p034:01031:leads in the same story. Often a writer creates a good lead and then repeats all
p034:01032:the information in the second paragraph, and again in the third.
p034:01033:   This is from an unpublished article on dog parks:
p034:01034:
p034:01035:      Dog parks are a great place to exercise and socialize your pet. They are
p034:01036:   often fenced in, so the dogs can safely run off-leash and play to their
p034:01037:   hearts’ content. As long as owners clean up after their pets and don’t allow
p034:01038:   aggressive behavior, a dog park can be a canine paradise.
p034:01039:      Buster the beagle was a shy, overweight couch potato before his owner
p034:01040:   discovered the Beagleville dog park. Thanks to the free dog-park manners
p034:01041:   classes offered by local volunteers, Buster lost his fear of other dogs as
p034:01042:   well as his excess weight. His daily visits to the dog park are the highlight
p035:01044:   whom he plays.
p035:01045:
p035:01046:   The second paragraph of that article would have made the better lead. It
p035:01047:contains all the information in the first paragraph plus a specific statement of
p035:01048:interest to the reader. If the writer had inserted the phrase “safe environment
p035:01049:and” before “well-trained pals” in the second paragraph, the first paragraph
p035:01050:could have been tossed in the wastebasket.

p035:01054:
p035:01055:3. Don’t Make Promises You Can’t Keep
p035:01056:
p035:01057:   Science has found a cure for cancer.
p035:01058:
p035:01059:   Is that a good lead? It is if science has found a cure for cancer. But it’s not a
p035:01060:good lead if the writer goes on to write, “Of course, nothing is definite yet,”
p035:01061:and “Dr. Inman’s theories have not been fully tested,” and “The serum has
p035:01062:never been tried on human beings.” Then the lead becomes a trick, a
p035:01063:dishonest way of getting the readers into the story, and they will feel cheated
p035:01064:after reading the article.
p035:01065:   A lead is not strong if it does not deliver on the promises it makes.
p035:01066:Anybody can write a lead that will attract attention, but if the lead is not
p035:01067:supported by what follows, it will do more harm than good.

p035:01072:4. Set a Tone and Maintain It
p035:01073:
p035:01074:   Almost every arena of activity conveys some message about the tone or
p035:01075:mood in which it is to be experienced. You are not expected to laugh out loud
p035:01076:at a hanging, but it’s okay to laugh at a Seinfeld rerun. You shouldn’t scream
p035:01077:when you find a great bargain at Macy’s, but it’s okay to scream at the
p035:01078:carnival when the Tilt-A-Whirl spins you around. (Throwing up is also more
p035:01079:acceptable there than at Macy’s.) In life, mixed messages about tone, such as
p035:01080:gag napkins at a wake, are disturbing. The same is true in reading.
p035:01081:   In your opening paragraph you set a tone. Your choice of words, your
p035:01082:arrangement of those words, and your choice of information all convey to the
p036:01084:makes an announcement such as “This is urgent,” or “Let’s be practical,” or
p036:01085:“Let’s laugh at this.”
p036:01086:   The following passage becomes “wrong” when the writer creates a sudden
p036:01087:and jarring shift in tone. He begins with a tone that is urgent, cruel, and
p036:01088:efficient, but he switches to a tone that is poetic, leisurely, and analytical.
p036:01089:Readers, believing they knew the writer’s attitude toward the material, are
p036:01090:suddenly not so sure!
p036:01091:
p036:01092:      Myron slammed the gate behind him and walked straight up to the cop
p036:01093:   on duty. “Now,” he said, “I want that scum now.” The cop moved quickly
p036:01094:   to block the door. But Myron was quicker. He rammed a fist into the cop’s
p036:01095:   gut, and when the cop keeled forward, Myron sliced a karate chop into the
p036:01096:   back of his neck. The cop dropped with a thud. Myron yanked open the
p036:01097:   door to the cellblock. He ran down the corridor to cell 9. He pulled open
p036:01098:   his jacket and grabbed the pistol from his holster. “Arrivederci, scum!” he
p036:01099:   cried at Demetrius. There was a pitiful shriek, the blast of gunfire, then the
p036:01100:   panicky screaming of other prisoners who feared a massacre.
p036:01101:      It was cool there in the cellblock, as cool as those distant mornings back
p036:01102:   in Trenton when Myron was a boy. The air here was light and refreshing,
p036:01103:   like a sparkling tonic brought in to douse the heat of the day. Even the
p036:01104:   cement walls around the cellblock had been painted a cool and soothing
p036:01105:   aqua, and on one end a mural of colorful birds enhanced the sense of
p036:01106:   calm. Myron was pleased with what he had done. For a moment he
p036:01107:   pretended that he was still sitting on the highest branch of that old
p036:01108:   cottonwood tree in Trenton, and he sipped slowly the heady air of success.

p036:01114:

p036:01118:wrong with writing three pages of junk before you get to information that
p036:01119:matters, as long as those three pages are extinct before the final draft. In other
p036:01120:words, don’t include your warm-up exercises with the manuscript.
p036:01121:   Study the beginning of your story carefully. You might discover that with

p039:01159:must be edited for print. When a newspaper editor has a seven-inch story that
p039:01160:he or she has to put into a six-inch hole in the newspaper, that editor doesn’t
p039:01161:run through the story with a pencil looking for useless adverbs or sentences
p039:01162:that can be rewritten. He or she simply cuts an inch off the bottom. That is
p039:01163:why each inch of a pyramid-style story should be less important than the inch
p039:01164:that came before it.

p039:01166:might be cut. And when you do, don’t put anything in paragraph 12 that the

p039:01184:2. Use Topic Sentences
p039:01185:
p039:01186:   A topic sentence contains the thought that is developed throughout the rest
p039:01187:of the paragraph. The topic sentence is commonly the first sentence in a
p039:01188:paragraph.
p040:01190:you first write a topic sentence. For each paragraph ask, “What do I want to
p040:01191:say here? What point do I want to make? What question do I want to
p040:01192:present?” Answer with a single general sentence. That is your topic sentence.
p040:01193:Chances are that the topic sentence will fall neatly into the paragraph it
p040:01194:inspires. But even if you don’t include the topic sentence in your paragraph, it
p040:01195:will serve as a guide. When you rewrite your early drafts, ask how each
p040:01196:sentence in a paragraph supports the topic sentence of the paragraph. If the
p040:01197:answer is “It doesn’t,” then ask what other work the sentence is doing in the
p040:01198:paragraph. If the answer is “None,” get rid of the sentence.
p040:01199:   Here are two paragraphs from books. Each one is the beginning of a section
p040:01200:or a chapter. The first paragraph begins with a topic sentence. The second
p040:01201:paragraph ends with a topic sentence. But note that in each one all the
p040:01202:sentences support or “prove” the statement made in the topic sentence.
p040:01203:
p040:01204:  Topic Sentence Begins Paragraph
p040:01205:Robin Phillips had a reputation as a theatrical wunderkind. There was very
p040:01206:little in the theatre to which he had not turned his hand at some time or
p040:01207:another, including acting, directing, and designing. He had a businessman’s
p040:01208:grasp of the financial side of theatre that was rare in an artist.
p040:01209:(from How Full of Briers: The Organizational Structure of the Non-Profit
p040:01210:Theatre Corporation)

p041:01233:4. Use Transitional Phrases
p041:01234:
p041:01235:   A transition in writing is a word or group of words that moves the reader
p041:01236:from one place to another. The “place” might be the location of a scene, a spot
p041:01237:in time, or an area of discussion. The transition should be quick, smooth,
p041:01238:quiet, reliable, and logical. And it should bring to itself a minimum of
p041:01239:attention.
p041:01240:   Transitions are important because they represent passage through a “danger
p041:01241:zone” where you risk losing your reader. You use a transition to show the
p041:01242:reader the connection between what he has just read and what he is about to
p041:01243:read by implying the relationship between those two bodies of information.
p041:01244:Here are some common transitional phrases:
p041:01245:
p041:01246:
p041:01247:           Transitions of Time                    Transitions of Place
p041:01248:
p041:01249:The following week . . .               On the other side of the mountain . . .
p041:01250:In December of that year . . .         In Black Eagle, Montana . . .
p041:01251:By the time Renaldo arrived . . .      Meanwhile, back at the ranch . . .
p041:01252:After the prom . . .                   When we got to Archie’s place . . .
p041:01253:Twenty years later . . .               From my house I could tell . . .
p041:01254:
p041:01255:  Transitions of Subject
p041:01256:Consequently . . .
p041:01257:In this manner . . .
p041:01258:On the other hand . . .
p041:01259:In contrast to . . .
p041:01260:Despite all this . . .
p041:01261:
p041:01262:  One other type of common transition occurs without words. It is the use of
p041:01263:spaces, such as skipping lines, starting new chapters, etc.
p042:01265:
p042:01266:   Writers often write long-winded and unnecessary transitions because they
p042:01267:are afraid that the short phrase hasn’t said enough. In the example below, you
p042:01268:can see how the writer slows down the story by trying to explain how Sam got
p042:01269:to the church when all he needs to do is acknowledge that Sam got from his
p042:01270:apartment to a church:
p042:01271:
p042:01272:   Sam moved slowly down the stairs of the apartment building. He walked
p042:01273:   across the street and climbed into his car. He turned the ignition key and
p042:01274:   put the car in gear. Then he pulled out into Maple Street traffic. When he
p042:01275:   reached Wilder Avenue, he took a left and drove for three blocks. At
p042:01276:   Warren Street he waited for a red light that seemed to take forever. Finally
p042:01277:   he got onto Carver. He could see the Bethany Church up ahead.
p042:01278:
p042:01279:  A better transitional sentence appears below:
p042:01280:
p042:01281:   Sam drove to the church.
p042:01282:
p042:01283:   Unless something important happened to Sam while driving his car over to
p042:01284:the church, don’t describe the drive. A transition is simply a bridge and should
p042:01285:be used to carry readers as quickly as possible from one place to the next.

p042:01289:6. Use Bridge Words
p042:01290:
p042:01291:   A bridge word is a word that is used in one paragraph and then repeated in
p042:01292:the following transition. It shows you how the writer got from one thought to
p042:01293:another, thus supplying you with a smooth bridge between thoughts.
p042:01294:   We use bridge words all the time to make conversations smooth. If your
p042:01295:friend says, “Let’s pick apples Saturday. My brother Larry lives in
p042:01296:California,” you will feel slightly jarred. You are distracted and will want to
p042:01297:ask, “Why are you suddenly talking about your brother?” On the other hand,
p042:01298:if your friend says, “Let’s pick apples Saturday. My brother Larry and I used
p042:01299:to pick apples all the time. He lives in California,” the word apples provides
p042:01300:you with a bridge across your friend’s thoughts, and you go along easily.
p043:01302:connection between subjects by using good bridge words.
p043:01303:   In High Stakes, I used the bridge word business to make a logical transition
p043:01304:into Bill Bennett’s change in status to a serious businessman in Las Vegas.
p043:01305:
p043:01306:      In 1965 Del Webb hired Bennett as a casino host at the Sahara in scenic
p043:01307:   Lake Tahoe, on the California-Nevada border, for $750 a month. There
p043:01308:   Bennett’s job was to squire high rollers around town and make sure their
p043:01309:   wheels were oiled. Flights had to be met; credit had to be arranged;
p043:01310:   restaurant tables had to be reserved. High rollers are a precious and
p043:01311:   delicate commodity in the gaming business, and Webb probably slept more
p043:01312:   easily at night knowing that they had been put into the hands of a man he
p043:01313:   knew and trusted.
p043:01314:      This was a job that required diplomacy, discretion, and great efficiency.
p043:01315:   But it was not a job that required Bill Bennett’s business sense. Still, it was
p043:01316:   a beginning, and from the beginning Bennett was convinced that he had
p043:01317:   stumbled onto a way to make more money than he had ever made in his
p043:01318:   life.

p043:01322:7. Avoid Wordiness
p043:01323:
p043:01324:   Wordiness has two meanings for the writer. You are wordy when you are
p043:01325:redundant, such as when you write, “Last May during the spring,” or “little
p043:01326:kittens,” or “very unique.”
p043:01327:   Wordiness for the writer also means using long words when there are good
p043:01328:short ones available, using uncommon words when familiar ones are handy,
p043:01329:or using words that look like the work of a Scrabble champion, not a writer.
p043:01330:   The following example of wordiness, which I’ve taken from a letter that
p043:01331:appeared in Dr. Adele M. Scheele’s “At Work” newspaper column, shows
p043:01332:how dull a writer becomes when he or she tries to impress a reader with
p043:01333:“intellectual” language.
p043:01334:
p043:01335:     In preparing a list of professional people whose opinion I respect, you
p043:01336:   are one of the first that comes to mind.

p043:01338:   heretofore been the case. . . .
p044:01340:book, but its greatest fault is wordiness.
p044:01341:   The overall tone of the letter is apologetic, meek, uncertain. The writer is
p044:01342:babbling. She’s trying to find words that are safe because they are vague and
p044:01343:they sound very professional to her. By trying to impress the reader with her
p044:01344:vocabulary, she is composing a letter that is almost incomprehensible.

p044:01366:8. Steal
p044:01367:
p044:01368:   Be a literary pack rat. Brighten up your story with a metaphor you read in
p044:01369:the Sunday paper. Make a point with an anecdote you heard at the barbershop.
p044:01370:Let a character tell a joke you heard in a bar. But steal small, not big, and
p044:01371:don’t steal from just one source. Someone once said that if you steal from one
p044:01372:writer, it’s called plagiarism, but if you steal from several, it’s called research.
p044:01373:So steal from everybody, but steal only a sentence or a phrase at a time. If you
p044:01374:use much more than that, you must get permission and then give credit. Here
p044:01375:are two examples of acceptable, honorable ways to steal.
p044:01376:   Whenever people ask me what I did for a living before I became a writer, I
p044:01377:reply, “I did all those crummy jobs that would someday look so glamorous on
p044:01378:the back of a book jacket.” It’s a cute line, one of many I use often in order to
p045:01380:invent the line. I read it twenty years ago in a TV Guide article by Merle
p045:01381:Miller, and I’ve used it ever since, rarely giving Miller credit for the line.
p045:01382:   The previous paragraph shows two examples of acceptable literary theft.
p045:01383:The first is Miller’s line, which the paragraph is about. The other is the
p045:01384:paragraph itself. It’s the opening paragraph for an article I wrote in Writer’s
p045:01385:Digest (April 1983) called “Do Editors Steal?” I stole it from myself.
p045:01386:   Thanks to the Internet, it is very difficult to get away with stealing any
p045:01387:significant amount of material or phrases. There are now plagiarism checkers
p045:01388:available for free online, and people use them.

p045:01393:9. Stop Writing When You Get to the End
p045:01394:
p045:01395:   A novel ends when your protagonist has solved his or her problem.
p045:01396:   An opinion piece ends when your opinion has been expressed.
p045:01397:   An instructional memo ends when the reader has been instructed.
p045:01398:   When you have done what you came to do, stop. Do not linger at the door
p045:01399:saying good-bye sixteen times.
p045:01400:   How do you know when you have finished? Look at the last sentence and
p045:01401:ask yourself, “What does the reader lose if I cross it out?” If the answer is
p045:01402:“nothing” or “I don’t know,” then cross it out. Do the same thing with the
p045:01403:next-to-last sentence, and so forth. When you get to the sentence that you
p045:01404:must have, read it out loud. Is it a good closing sentence? Does it sound final?
p045:01405:Is it pleasant to the ear? Does it leave the reader in the mood you intended? If
p045:01406:so, you are done. If not, rewrite it so that it does. Then stop writing.

p047:01453:2. Listen to What You Write
p047:01454:
p047:01455:  Writing is not a visual art any more than composing music is a visual art.
p047:01456:  To write is to create music. The words you write make sounds, and when
p047:01457:those sounds are in harmony, the writing will work.
p048:01459:Hungarian Rhapsody No. 2, or it might sound like “Satisfaction.” You decide.
p048:01460:But give it unity. It should not sound like a musical battle between the
p048:01461:Cleveland Symphony Orchestra and the Rolling Stones.
p048:01462:    Read aloud what you write and listen to its music. Listen for dissonance.
p048:01463:Listen for the beat. Listen for gaps where the music leaps from sound to
p048:01464:sound instead of flowing as it should. Listen for sour notes. Is this word a
p048:01465:little sharp, is that one a bit flat? Listen for instruments that don’t blend well.
p048:01466:Is there an electric guitar shrieking amid the whispers of flutes and violins?
p048:01467:Imagine the sound of each word as an object falling onto the eardrum. Does it
p048:01468:make a soft landing like the word ripple, or does it land hard and dig in like
p048:01469:inexorable? Does it cut off all sound for an instant, like brutal, or does it
p048:01470:massage the reader’s ear, like melodious?
p048:01471:    There are no good sounds or bad sounds, just as there are no good notes or
p048:01472:bad notes in music. It is the way in which you combine them that can make
p048:01473:the writing succeed or fail. It’s the music that matters.

p048:01478:3. Mimic Spoken Language
p048:01479:
p048:01480:   Writing should be conversational. That does not mean that your writing
p048:01481:should be an exact duplicate of speech; it should not. Your writing should
p048:01482:convey to the reader a sense of conversation. It should furnish the immediacy
p048:01483:and the warmth of a personal conversation.
p048:01484:   Most real conversations, if committed to paper, would dull the senses.
p048:01485:Conversations stumble; they stray; they repeat; they are bloated with, you
p048:01486:know, like, meaningless words; and they are often cut short by intrusions. But
p048:01487:what they have going for them is human contact, the sound of a human voice.
p048:01488:   And if you can put that quality into your writing, you will get the reader’s
p048:01489:attention.
p048:01490:   So mimic spoken language in the variety of its music, in the simplicity of
p048:01491:its words, in the directness of its expression. But do not forfeit the enormous
p048:01492:advantages of the written word. Writing provides time for contemplation. Use
p048:01493:it well.
p048:01494:   In conversation the perfect word is not always there. In writing we can try
p048:01495:out fifteen different words before we are satisfied.
p048:01496:   In conversation we spread our thoughts thin. In writing we can compress.
p049:01498:an ordinary conversation. Make it a good one.

p049:01503:4. Vary Sentence Length
p049:01504:
p049:01505:   This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word
p049:01506:sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is
p049:01507:happening. The writing is getting boring. The sound of it drones. It’s like a
p049:01508:stuck record. The ear demands some variety. Now listen. I vary the sentence
p049:01509:length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings. It has a pleasant rhythm,
p049:01510:a lilt, a harmony. I use short sentences. And I use sentences of medium length.
p049:01511:And sometimes when I am certain the reader is rested, I will engage him with
p049:01512:a sentence of considerable length, a sentence that burns with energy and
p049:01513:builds with all the impetus of a crescendo, the roll of the drums, the crash of
p049:01514:the cymbals—sounds that say, “Listen to this; it is important.”
p049:01515:   So write with a combination of short, medium, and long sentences. Create a
p049:01516:sound that pleases the reader’s ear. Don’t just write words. Write music.

p054:01708:10. Don’t Force a Personal Style
p054:01709:

p054:01711:clothes. Style is your writing. It is inexorably knotted to the content of your

p056:01765:2. Use Dense Words
p056:01766:
p056:01767:   A dense word is a word that crowds a lot of meaning into a small space.
p056:01768:The fewer words you use to express an idea, the more impact that idea will
p056:01769:have. When you revise, look for opportunities to cross out several words and
p056:01770:insert one. Once a month is monthly; something new is novel; people they
p056:01771:didn’t know are strangers; and something impossible to imagine is
p056:01772:inconceivable.
p057:01774:
p057:01775:   Do you know what a mandible is? Your dentist does. She uses that word
p057:01776:every day.
p057:01777:   So if you are writing a story just for your dentist, use mandible. But if you
p057:01778:are writing for everybody else, use the more familiar word, jaw.
p057:01779:   A word that your reader doesn’t recognize has no power. If it confuses the
p057:01780:reader and sends him or her scurrying for the dictionary, it has broken the
p057:01781:reader’s spell.
p057:01782:   Familiar words have power. By avoiding very long words, you avoid most
p057:01783:of the words that your reader doesn’t know. But you should also replace short
p057:01784:words if they are so rare that your reader might not know them.
p057:01785:   Even though delegate is longer than depute, it is better. Don’t write
p057:01786:sclerous if you can write hardened, and if you have written that something is
p057:01787:virescent, please go back and say that it is turning green.
p057:01788:   A couple of tips. A word is familiar if it came easily to you but is not part
p057:01789:of some specialized knowledge you have, such as a botanical term. A word is
p057:01790:unfamiliar if you never heard of it until you found it in the thesaurus or if you
p057:01791:haven’t read it at least three times in the past year.

p057:01796:
p057:01797:   Active verbs do something. Inactive verbs are something. You will gain

p058:01814:   Verbs, words of action, are the primary source of energy in your sentences.
p058:01815:They are the executives; they should be in charge. All other parts of speech
p058:01816:are valuable assistants, but if your verbs are weak, all the modifiers in the
p058:01817:world won’t save your story from dullness.

p058:01819:active, or are unnecessarily dependent on adverbs for their meaning.

p058:01821:for you than any other part of speech. Strong verbs will reduce the number of
p058:01822:words in your sentences by eliminating many adverbs. And, more important,
p058:01823:strong verbs will pack your paragraphs with the energy, the excitement, and
p058:01824:the sense of motion that readers crave.

p060:01908:7. Use the Active Voice . . . Most of the Time
p060:01909:
p060:01910:   When a verb is in the active voice, the subject of the sentence is also the
p060:01911:doer of the action.
p060:01912:   The sentence “John picked up the bag” is in the active voice because the
p060:01913:subject, John, is also the thing or person doing the action of “picking up.”
p060:01914:   The sentence “The bag was picked up by John” is in the passive voice
p060:01915:because the subject of the sentence, bag, is the passive receiver of the action.
p060:01916:   Generally the active voice makes for more interesting reading, and it is the
p060:01917:active voice that you should cultivate as your normal writing habit. The active
p060:01918:voice strikes more directly at the thought you want to express, it is generally
p060:01919:shorter, and it holds the reader closer to what you write because it creates a
p060:01920:stronger sense that “something is happening.”
p060:01921:   Listen to how the following passive voice sentences are improved when
p060:01922:they are turned into the active voice.

p060:01928:book is about.                            prints.

p061:01936:you will want to emphasize it by mentioning it first. When that’s the case, you
p061:01937:will use the passive voice.

p061:01939:things that happened to your car. In the active voice it would look like this:

p061:01956:happened to it has a flow and rhythm lacking in the first example.

p064:02062:

p065:02091:attention to. They contain the information you are most eager to
p065:02092:communicate. You can get that extra attention for those words by placing
p065:02093:them at the end of the sentence.

p065:02095:write, “Some redwoods are more than 350 feet tall.” But if you want to
p065:02096:emphasize the fact that one of the attractions in California is the redwood
p065:02097:trees, you would write, “Also found in California are the 350-foot redwood
p065:02098:trees.”

p065:02102:June first.” And if you want to emphasize who the check is to go to, write,
p065:02103:“On June first the check for $107.12 should be sent to me.”

p067:02131:   In order to write successfully, you don’t have to become a great writer. But

p067:02133:product, take your advice, mail a check, or worry about the problem you

p067:02135:share a private moment with the readers. Present yourself to readers as

p067:02137:conversationally, and strive always to present in your writing some honest
p067:02138:picture of who you are.
p067:02139:   Readers will like you if you edit from your work French phrases, obscure
p067:02140:literary allusions, and archaic words that are known to only six persons in the
p067:02141:world.

p067:02143:their world is like. If you write an article called “Getting Back on the Budget”
p067:02144:for Woman’s Day, and you begin by advising the readers to go out and borrow
p067:02145:$100,000, you will reveal your ignorance of the readers’ financial status. The
p067:02146:readers won’t like you. (And of course the editors at Woman’s Day won’t like
p067:02147:you and won’t publish your article.)

p067:02149:course, there are times when humor is inappropriate (on a death certificate,
p067:02150:for example), but don’t hesitate to bring humor into your business
p067:02151:correspondence and articles.

p067:02153:for example, you might write, “This third step is a little hard to master. I

p067:02160:2. Write About People
p067:02161:
p067:02162:  People are why TVs get turned on. People are why books get opened.
p067:02163:People are why magazines are purchased. And people are why the well-told
p068:02165:   People is the one subject that everybody cares about.
p068:02166:   What do other people think? How do they act? What makes them angry,
p068:02167:happy, enthusiastic? How will they vote in the next election? How can I get
p068:02168:them to fall in love with me, buy my product, support my plan? These are the
p068:02169:questions readers ask.
p068:02170:   So try to put humanity into everything you write. There are times when you
p068:02171:cannot comfortably dress your prose in flesh and blood, but those times are
p068:02172:rare. Even a how-to article is about a person named “you.”
p068:02173:   Don’t write about the new bookkeeping system. Write about how the new
p068:02174:bookkeeping system will affect people.

p068:02176:one family that lives in a car because they cannot pay rent out of their small
p068:02177:welfare check.
p068:02178:   If you are writing a blog to attract new members to your church, don’t write
p068:02179:about the steeple and the organ. Write about the people who come to church
p068:02180:suppers, the people who volunteer for committees, the people your readers
p068:02181:will meet if they show up for church on Sunday.

p078:02508:
p078:02509:    To succeed as a writer, you must respect the rules of grammar. If editors or
p078:02510:teachers have consistently found grammatical errors in your writing, the flaw
p078:02511:in your work is not minor. It is fatal. Good writing and good grammar are not
p078:02512:twins, but they are usually found in the same place.
p078:02513:    The rules of grammar exist to help you write well, not to sabotage your
p078:02514:work, and you cannot write well without them. The rules of grammar organize
p078:02515:the language just as the rules of arithmetic organize the world of numbers.
p078:02516:Imagine how difficult math would be if three and three equaled six only once
p078:02517:in a while or if a tenth was equal to ten percent only when somebody felt like
p078:02518:it. Grammatical rules about tense, gender, number, person, and case provide
p078:02519:us with a literary currency that we can spend wherever English is spoken or
p078:02520:read. Grammar is a system of rules for speaking and writing a given language,
p078:02521:and that system was not created just so that English teachers would have
p078:02522:something to harass you about. It exists so that we can communicate well, and
p078:02523:when you blatantly violate portions of the system, you are chipping away at
p078:02524:the stability of the whole.
p078:02525:    While you should accept the fact that changes do occur in the language,
p078:02526:you should also resist each change every step of the way. Change should not
p078:02527:come easily. New words and constructions seeking entry into the language
p078:02528:should be met by a mighty army of grammarians saying, “It’s wrong,” and the
p078:02529:rest of us saying, “It just doesn’t sound right,” so that the trendy, the
p078:02530:senseless, and the merely pretty fall dead on the battlefield, and only the truly
p078:02531:valuable survive. Change, if I might switch my metaphor, should come
p078:02532:gradually and rarely, or the language will fall like a table that has all its legs
p078:02533:removed at once instead of replaced one at a time.

p082:02685:8. Beware These Common Mistakes
p082:02686:
p082:02687:   In addition to major grammatical mistakes, there are a good many minor
p082:02688:mistakes to be made, and nobody’s hands are completely clean. We all have a
p082:02689:few grammatical rules that we can never quite nail down despite our “ears”
p082:02690:for language. Many people cannot remember the difference between who and
p082:02691:whom. (Who is typically the subject of a sentence and whom is an object that
p082:02692:follows a preposition such as to, with, for, or about. “Who is going to the
p082:02693:prom with you, and with whom did she go last year?”) Many writers use like
p082:02694:as a conjunction (She walks like she’s got a train to catch), even though most
p082:02695:grammarians insist it be used only as a preposition (It looks like a luxury car,
p082:02696:and it rides like a dream). And almost everybody gets confused about lay and
p082:02697:lie. (And with good reason. Lay means to put something down, and lie means
p082:02698:to recline, but the past tense of lie is, would you believe, lay.)
p082:02699:   Another tricky distinction to remember is between was and were. Use was
p082:02700:when you’re talking about a fact or possible fact: “If he was afraid, I couldn’t
p082:02701:tell” (I don’t know if he was afraid or not) and were when there is no
p082:02702:possibility of fact (Beyoncé sings “If I Were a Boy” because she is not a boy).
p083:02704:confusion. That is used to introduce a restrictive clause. A restrictive clause
p083:02705:“restricts” the meaning of your noun and can’t be removed from the sentence
p083:02706:without subtracting some of its sense. “I love the book that Alex gave me”
p083:02707:restricts us to, or identifies, the specific book: the one Alex gave me. A
p083:02708:nonrestrictive clause is a piece of extra information introduced by which. For
p083:02709:example, “I love the book, which Alex gave me” assumes you know which
p083:02710:book we’re talking about but adds the detail that it came from Alex. You’ll
p083:02711:usually see a comma prior to a nonrestrictive clause.
p083:02712:   This distinction is a good general rule of thumb, but it’s so common to see
p083:02713:which used with a restrictive clause (“Students protested the rule which
p083:02714:prohibits gum chewing”) that you’ll never get dinged for it.
p083:02715:   Minor mistakes like these might confuse, disturb, or disgust your reader,
p083:02716:depending on which mistakes you make, how often you make them, and who
p083:02717:the reader is. It’s arbitrary. When I wear an editor’s hat, I don’t mind a writer
p083:02718:using who instead of whom, or occasionally using like as a conjunction. On
p083:02719:the other hand, I would be inclined to reject the writer who consistently used
p083:02720:like as a conjunction (even though Shakespeare did it) or who wrote, “I lied
p083:02721:down for a nap.”
p083:02722:   When it comes to minor grammatical mistakes, readers don’t all draw lines
p083:02723:at the same place. But all readers have a limited number of grammatical
p083:02724:mistakes that they will forgive, so you should at least aim for grammatical
p083:02725:perfection except when you can improve the writing by breaking a rule of
p083:02726:grammar.
p083:02727:   Many grammatical errors occur because the writer tries too hard not to
p083:02728:make a mistake. Instead of trusting his or her ear for language, the writer
p083:02729:reacts to some traumatic correction in childhood. Most of us, for example,
p083:02730:once said, “Jimmy and me are going to the movies,” and had some impolite
p083:02731:adult snap at us, “It’s ‘Jimmy and I are going to the movies.’” Many people
p083:02732:were corrected so often that they now change all their me’s to I’s and write
p083:02733:things like “The contract was given to Jimmy and I.” (It should be “Jimmy
p083:02734:and me.” I is nominative, me is objective.) Or, having been bawled out for
p083:02735:saying, “I played bad,” when he should have said, “I played badly,” the kid
p083:02736:turns into an adult who writes things like “I feel badly about your loss,” when
p083:02737:he means that he feels bad about your loss. The problem is that the writer
p083:02738:recalls the specific words involved instead of the pertinent rule, which was
p083:02739:not explained.
p084:02741:
p084:02742:   Even if you know all the rules of grammar, you’re covered only for today,
p084:02743:not tomorrow. The rules change. Grammar is a living thing; it grows to meet
p084:02744:new needs.
p084:02745:   An obvious example of this is something called the degenderization of
p084:02746:language. The feminist movement has successfully lifted our consciousness
p084:02747:about the fact that English pronouns of unspecified gender are always male, a
p084:02748:fact that contributes to the idea that males are the regular folk and females are
p084:02749:something else. It is good grammar but poor feminism to write, “A doctor
p084:02750:should always clean his stethoscope before checking someone’s heart.”
p084:02751:   Several solutions to the problem have been suggested. Among them are
p084:02752:he/she, his/her, and s/he. None has really caught on. What is catching on,
p084:02753:however, is “A doctor should always clean their stethoscope,” an error in
p084:02754:number that is perpetrated by people who would rather offend grammarians
p084:02755:than feminists. It’s good feminism but bad grammar, and I don’t like it. In
p084:02756:fact, to be perfectly honest about it, I hate it. But I’m starting to get used to it,
p084:02757:and it seems to be earning its way into the language. If it proves to be made of
p084:02758:hearty stuff, I will welcome it.
p084:02759:   The point is that it’s bad grammar today, but it might be good grammar ten
p084:02760:years from now. Today’s rules have no better shot at immortality than thee
p084:02761:and thou had.
p084:02762:

p084:02764:
p084:02765:10. Prefer Good Writing to Good Grammar
p084:02766:
p084:02767:  Keep in mind that good grammar, even perfect grammar, does not
p084:02768:guarantee good writing any more than a good referee guarantees a good
p084:02769:basketball game.
p084:02770:  “It is my objective to utilize my management expertise more fully, than has
p084:02771:heretofore been the case” is acceptable grammar but poor writing because it is
p084:02772:poor communication. The sentence should read, “I’m looking for a better
p084:02773:job.” On the other hand, “I ain’t got no money” is terrible grammar but could
p084:02774:be good writing in some context by communicating exactly what the writer
p084:02775:wants to communicate.
p084:02776:  There are many writing situations in which inferior grammar makes for
p084:02777:superior writing. You could use poor grammar to reveal the character of a
p085:02779:
p085:02780:   The thing was, we had gone fishing that day and Pa had wore himself out
p085:02781:   with it the way he usually did when he went fishing. I mean he went at it
p085:02782:   pretty hard and called the fish all sorts of names—he lost one pretty nice
p085:02783:   one and hopped up in the boat and banged the pole down in the water
p085:02784:   which was about enough to scare a big-sized alligator away, much less a
p085:02785:   fish, and he spent most of the afternoon after that cussing and ranting at
p085:02786:   everything that happened.
p085:02787:
p085:02788:   You could use poor grammar in an essay or an opinion piece to establish a
p085:02789:certain tone: “Marvin Hagler and Ray Leonard go at each other tonight in the
p085:02790:Centrum, and it ain’t gonna be pretty.”
p085:02791:   You can also use faulty grammar in a story or novel to characterize people,
p085:02792:places, and events, or to establish a casual, conversational tone. In this
p085:02793:example, poor grammar does both jobs: “Moose asked every guy in the bar if
p085:02794:they had seen Helen. Nobody knew nothing. Moose looked like he was going
p085:02795:to tear the place apart.” There are three grammatical mistakes in those three
p085:02796:sentences, but they are all intentional and they are all doing some work.
p085:02797:   Whenever you knowingly use poor grammar, you should ask yourself two
p085:02798:questions. The first: Is my meaning clear? If the answer is no, rewrite. The
p085:02799:second question: What am I getting in return for the poor grammar? If you
p085:02800:can’t answer that, don’t use poor grammar.
p085:02801:   So strive most of all for good writing, but make proper grammar your rule
p085:02802:and improper grammar your exception. Don’t give easy access to every
p085:02803:bizarre construction or chunk of senseless jargon that comes whistling down
p085:02804:the pike. Never violate a rule of grammar unless you have a good reason, one
p085:02805:that improves the writing.
p085:02806:   But never choose good grammar over good writing. There is nothing
p085:02807:virtuous about good grammar that does not work. Your goal is good writing.
p085:02808:Good grammar is only one of the tools you use to achieve it.

p087:02850:2. Know When to Use a Comma
p087:02851:
p087:02852:  Commas are used to add clarity to a sentence. Consider the sentence below:
p087:02853:
p087:02854:   She was frightened when he kissed her and fainted.
p088:02856:kissed her. On the other hand, perhaps she became frightened because he
p088:02857:fainted during the kiss. Only a comma will give this sentence meaning for us:
p088:02858:
p088:02859:   She was frightened when he kissed her, and fainted.
p088:02860:
p088:02861:Ah, she fainted.
p088:02862:   When deciding whether a sentence you have written needs a comma, read
p088:02863:the sentence out loud. Is a pause needed for clarity? Read the sentence
p088:02864:without the pause—quickly, if you’re still not certain. If the sentence makes
p088:02865:perfect sense to you when read at breakneck speed, banish that comma. In
p088:02866:addition to being wrong, overpunctuation is deadly dull. A good piece of
p088:02867:advice: When your ear fails you and you can’t decide whether to add that
p088:02868:comma, DON’T.
p088:02869:   Many otherwise good writers use too many commas. I think one of the
p088:02870:reasons is because we were half-asleep in grammar classes as children and
p088:02871:never bothered to learn the difference between restrictive and nonrestrictive
p088:02872:clauses. Unfortunately, while we must have been sleeping when the terms
p088:02873:were explained, we learned just enough to get ourselves in trouble.
p088:02874:   Read the sentences below and see which ones require commas:
p088:02875:
p088:02876:  1. My friend Pat goes to law school.
p088:02877:  2. A dance like the limbo requires a broomstick or pole.
p088:02878:  3. Animals that have fur are fun to pet.
p088:02879:  4. Do not use a comma unless a pause is needed for clarity.
p088:02880:
p088:02881:The answer? None of the sentences needs a comma. If you read the sentences
p088:02882:out loud, your ears should have told you that pauses were not needed. But if
p088:02883:you were once one of those children who slept half the time during grammar
p088:02884:classes, you might have decided to add commas because the sentences looked
p088:02885:a lot like sentences that need commas.
p088:02886:   Restrictive clauses and words do not require commas. Nonrestrictive words
p088:02887:and clauses do. Restrictive elements define and limit a sentence. They must
p088:02888:be present for a sentence to retain its intended meaning. Nonrestrictive
p088:02889:elements, which are parenthetical, do not.
p088:02890:   Look at the following sentences. Notice that in each of the sentences with
p088:02891:nonrestrictive elements, the material contained in commas could be removed
p088:02892:without changing the sentence’s meaning.
p089:02894:
p089:02895:Some dances, like the limbo, require       A dance like the limbo requires a
p089:02896:broomsticks or poles.                      broomstick or pole.
p089:02897:One of my friends, Pat, goes to law        My friend Pat goes to law school.
p089:02898:school.
p089:02899:Snakes, which don’t have fur, aren’t much Animals that have fur are fun to pet.
p089:02900:fun to pet.

p089:02903:
p089:02904:   1. Use a comma following introductory words like Yes, No, and But.
p089:02905:Realize, though, that there are times when such words are not being used as
p089:02906:introductions to a sentence.
p089:02907:
p089:02908:
p089:02909:                 Wrong                                         Right
p089:02910:
p089:02911:Yes I did take the money.                  Yes, I did take the money.

p089:02913:Now, is the time for all good men to come Now is the time for all good men to come
p089:02914:to the aid of their country.              to the aid of their country.
p089:02915:Now if you take the bus, you’ll save       Now, if you take the bus, you’ll save
p089:02916:money.                                     money.

p089:02918:  2. Clauses joined by but often require a comma: He wanted to eat out, but

p096:03118:of shorthand or code. Police officers, venture capitalists, sailors, surgeons,
p096:03119:surfers, and the military all have their own jargon, which might be slang

p097:03160:3. Avoid Parentheses
p097:03161:
p097:03162:    Parentheses are used to enclose material that is not strictly necessary but
p097:03163:rather serves as a comment on or a supplement to the main text. If you are
p097:03164:using parentheses more than three times in a ten-page story, you are either
p097:03165:interrupting the reader too much or you are using parentheses unnecessarily.
p097:03166:The writer usually turns to parentheses out of laziness, not out of need, and
p097:03167:there is usually an unobtrusive way to include the information without
p097:03168:parentheses.

p097:03170:does not know how to write English or is too indolent to take the trouble to do
p097:03171:it; . . . a man who will wantonly use a parenthesis will steal. For these reasons
p097:03172:I am unfriendly to the parenthesis. When a man puts one into my mouth, his
p097:03173:life is no longer safe.”
p097:03174:    Twain, as you can see, didn’t much care for the parenthesis.

p098:03202:5. Don’t Use Transitions to Conceal Information
p098:03203:
p098:03204:  As a writer, you have entered a covenant with the reader. The whole
p098:03205:writing/reading process depends on the writer and reader having faith that the
p098:03206:other will not violate the terms of the covenant. If the covenant were written
p098:03207:out, it would contain a clause concerning transitions that would look like this:
p098:03208:
p098:03209:      Reader agrees that a transition such as “Sam drove to the church” can
p098:03210:   encompass all the routine acts of starting a car, taking left turns, etc.
p098:03211:      In turn Writer agrees not to use such transitions to deprive reader of
p098:03212:   information that belongs to Reader.
p098:03213:
p098:03214:    In other words, you shouldn’t use the transition “Sam drove to the church”
p098:03215:if later in your story you are going to mention that Sam had a horrible
p098:03216:accident and killed a carload of lawyers while driving to the church.
p098:03217:    Don’t cheat readers on the grounds that you wish to surprise them later in
p098:03218:the story. Readers know the difference between being cheated and being
p098:03219:surprised.
p099:03221:
p099:03222:   Many writers try to force transitions to do work that should have been done
p099:03223:elsewhere in the story or article.
p099:03224:   In the 1930’s, adventure serials with cliff-hanger endings were popular in
p099:03225:the London monthlies. At the end of one installment, the writer had his hero,
p099:03226:Ben, trapped at the bottom of a dark and slippery twenty-foot pit with no
p099:03227:tools, no ladder, and nobody around to hear his cries. For a month people all
p099:03228:over London discussed Ben’s plight. How would good old Ben get out of this
p099:03229:mess? Unfortunately, the writer of the serial was wondering the same thing,
p099:03230:and by the time his deadline arrived he had not come up with a clever
p099:03231:solution. So he began his episode with, “After Ben got out of the pit, he
p099:03232:proceeded to walk toward the city,” a transition that put his readers into a
p099:03233:lynching mood and did serious damage to the writer’s career.
p099:03234:   “After Ben got out of the pit” would be a perfectly decent transition if
p099:03235:climbing out of a twenty-foot pit were as routine an activity as driving to a
p099:03236:church. The reader will accept your acknowledgment of changes in time or
p099:03237:place only if those changes could have been accomplished in normal, routine
p099:03238:ways, or in unusual ways that you have made believable earlier in your story.
p099:03239:If you tell us on page 1 that Superman can fly, we will have no trouble later
p099:03240:on accepting the transition “On Tuesday Superman flew to Clinton,
p099:03241:Massachusetts.”
p099:03242:   When you find yourself having difficulty moving from one section of an
p099:03243:article to the next, the problem might be due to the fact that you are leaving
p099:03244:out information. Rather than trying to force an awkward transition, take
p099:03245:another look at what you have written and ask yourself what you need to
p099:03246:explain in order to move on to your next section.

p099:03251:

p099:03253:mean you should write everything in the first person, or that you should
p099:03254:meticulously insert your feelings and observations into every memo. What it
p099:03255:does mean is you should not be afraid to climb onto the page when your
p099:03256:presence will strengthen what you have written.
p100:03258:you were staying in the hotel at the time. It would be unthinkable to leave

p100:03260:you saw the people leap from windows, you heard the lower floors cave in

p100:03265:   floors above a flame that would eventually consume the entire hotel and

p100:03269:were assigned three months later to write a story about it, your experience at

p101:03316:9. Don’t Play Word Games
p101:03317:
p101:03318:   Puns, double entendres, rhymes, inside jokes, and various other literary

p101:03322:success of the writing. The reader sees too much of form and loses track of
p101:03323:content. Quite often the reader will chuckle at your cleverness but will cease
p101:03324:to take your story seriously.

p102:03353:11. Don’t Play the Mystery Game
p102:03354:
p102:03355:   You are playing the mystery game when you withhold some vital piece of
p102:03356:information because you think the reader will hang in there until the end just
p102:03357:to find out what it is. More likely the reader will be distracted by the
p102:03358:unanswered question and will not pay close attention to what you write.
p102:03359:   In On Becoming a Novelist, John Gardner put it this way: “One of the most
p102:03360:common mistakes among young writers is the idea that a story gets its power
p102:03361:from withheld information—that is, from the writer setting the reader up and
p102:03362:then bushwhacking him. Ungenerous fiction is first and foremost fiction in
p102:03363:which the writer is unwilling to take the reader as an equal partner.”
p102:03364:   Every writing teacher has read hundreds of unpublished stories that begin
p102:03365:with something like “The package arrived at two a.m. Sammy opened it in a
p102:03366:frenzy; he stared at its contents and smiled.” The story goes on and on, and
p102:03367:we hear a lot about “it” or “the contents of the package.” If we stick around
p102:03368:long enough, we discover in the last paragraph that the package contained an
p102:03369:adorable beagle puppy.
p102:03370:   Another common version of the mystery game occurs in the first-person
p102:03371:story. We don’t learn until the end that the speaker is not a person at all but a
p102:03372:1957 nickel or an oak tree. This is silly stuff. Gardner describes it as the
p103:03374:extremely efficient way to drive away readers.
p103:03375:   Similarly, when writing a paper for a school assignment, bring the
p103:03376:statement you wish to prove into the paper’s beginning, and then prove your
p103:03377:statement. Don’t wait until you are almost finished with your paper to
p103:03378:mention what it is you are writing about.

p106:03458:   Before you turn in anything you have written—whether to a teacher or an
p106:03459:editor—read aloud every word.
p106:03460:   Often when you write and rewrite and constantly rearrange information,
p106:03461:your ear for the sound of the writing becomes corrupted. Reading out loud
p106:03462:will return to you the true sound of your story. You will hear the sour note of
p106:03463:the word that’s “just not right,” and the drastic changes in tone will cry out to
p106:03464:you for editing. You’ll notice that you are breathless at the end of one long
p106:03465:sentence, and you will know that you must break it up into two or three.
p106:03466:Listen for the music, variety, and emphasis of your sentences. You will
p106:03467:discover that some of them are confusing and need a word added or removed
p106:03468:for clarity’s sake. And you will see that a sentence like “Who knew that Lou
p106:03469:cued Sue, too?” might not look funny, but it sure makes a funny and
p106:03470:distracting noise in the reader’s head.

p106:03475:
p106:03476:   Let’s pretend your mechanic called you up and said, “Mr. Duckworth
p106:03477:(assuming your name is Mr. Duckworth), your car is ready. I’ve put in a new
p106:03478:carburetor, an alternator, and three unnecessary parts.”
p106:03479:   What would you think of such a phone call?
p106:03480:   Hmmm. You might think it’s time to get a new mechanic.
p106:03481:   You don’t want unnecessary parts in your car. They do no good, and they
p106:03482:slow you down.
p106:03483:   So you certainly don’t want unnecessary words in your writing. They do no
p106:03484:good, and they slow you and your reader down.
p106:03485:   Every word you write should be doing some work in the sentence. It should
p106:03486:earn its keep by providing some portion of the total information you are trying
p106:03487:to communicate. A word is unnecessary if it’s doing no work, if it’s doing
p106:03488:work that doesn’t have to be done, or if it’s doing work that’s being done by
p106:03489:another word or phrase nearby.

p107:03518:3. Think About What You Have Written
p107:03519:
p107:03520:   It’s very easy when you are locked in the passionate embrace of the writing
p107:03521:muse to write something that sounds really dumb. The writer routinely
p107:03522:includes the banal, the inaccurate, and the just plain stupid in early drafts
p107:03523:simply by forgetting that what one meant to say is not always what got
p107:03524:written down.
p107:03525:   But when the passion cools a bit and the writer rereads his or her work,
p107:03526:curious phrases will suddenly show themselves: Did she mean to write,
p107:03527:“There was literally an ocean of people . . .”? Of course not. But she did. Did
p107:03528:he intend to write, “He could care less . . .”? No. He meant to write, “He
p107:03529:couldn’t care less.”
p108:03531:complete a final draft, let at least a day pass and then think carefully about
p108:03532:what you wrote before turning to your keyboard. You may find that what you
p108:03533:thought was brilliant prose on Tuesday borders on the moronic by Friday. On
p108:03534:the other hand, you may discover that what seemed trivial when you wrote it
p108:03535:is, in fact, profound.

p108:03540:4. Ask Yourself These Questions
p108:03541:
p108:03542:  Before calling a draft final, ask these questions:
p108:03543:
p108:03544:  1. Is it clear from the beginning what you’re writing about?
p108:03545:  2. Does each paragraph advance the subject?
p108:03546:  3. Do the important ideas stand out clearly?
p108:03547:  4. Are more details, examples, or anecdotes needed?
p108:03548:  5. Is the information sufficiently clear?
p108:03549:  6. Are there sweeping statements that need to be supported?
p108:03550:  7. Do any technical terms need explanation?
p108:03551:  8. Is there needless repetition?
p108:03552:  9. Is the tone consistent?
p108:03553: 10. Are any of the sentences too involved to follow with ease?
p108:03554: 11. Are any of the words vague?
p108:03555: 12. Are there grammatical errors?
p108:03556: 13. Are there punctuation errors?

p108:03561:5. Follow These Rules of Form for Titles
p108:03562:
p108:03563:  Before submitting a final manuscript, review these rules for titles:
p108:03564:
p108:03565:  1. The title of a book-length manuscript should be typed on a separate
p108:03566:     page, together with the author’s name. Set the title at the very center of
p108:03567:     the page. The author’s name goes below the title.
p108:03568:  2. Titles of short manuscripts should be centered at the top of the first page
p108:03569:     of your paper or story.
p109:03571:           Educated: A Memoir
p109:03572:           Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone
p109:03573:           Of Mice and Men
p109:03574:     Prepositions that have fewer than four letters should not be capitalized
p109:03575:     unless they are the first word of the title. Similarly, only capitalize
p109:03576:     definite and indefinite articles if they are the first word of the title.
p109:03577:  4. Use italics for titles of books, magazines, movies, and plays.
p109:03578:  5. Use quotation marks around the titles of articles, short stories, poems,
p109:03579:     songs, and other short pieces of writing.

p109:03584:6. Prepare a Perfect Manuscript
p109:03585:
p109:03586:   If you are proud of the words you have written, you will want to present

p109:03602:7. Use Common Sense
p109:03603:
p109:03604:   I write often about writing, and that can be terrifying. Sometimes I feel as if
p109:03605:I’m standing in front of a firing squad and the captain will give the order to
p109:03606:shoot as soon as I have violated my own advice. Have I used too many words
p109:03607:to tell you not to use too many words? Is my voice too passive when I tell you
p109:03608:to use the active voice? Is my grammar faulty when I tell you to bone up on
p109:03609:your grammar?
p110:03611:scanning my every word with a magnifying glass, all of them poised to leap
p110:03612:on the first sign of contradiction. Off to their laptops they will run, and soon
p110:03613:my inbox will be overflowing with e-mail, all of which begin, “Dear Mr.
p110:03614:Provost, On page such and such you said this, but just thirty-two pages later
p110:03615:you said that. Are you a moron?”
p110:03616:   No, I’m not. Honestly. I am—dare I say it—an artist. And that is my escape
p110:03617:hatch. Writing is art, not science, and when I finish a piece of writing, I do not
p110:03618:review every single one of my tips. I ask: Have I communicated well? Have I
p110:03619:pleased my readers? Have I given them something that is a joy to read? Have
p110:03620:I entertained them, informed them, persuaded them, and made my thoughts
p110:03621:clear to them? Have I given them what they wanted?
p110:03622:   And these are the questions you must ask about all that you write. If the
p110:03623:answers are yes, you have succeeded. If the answers are no, you have failed.
p110:03624:Writing well is what counts.
p110:03625:   The tips in this book encompass much of the accumulated knowledge about
p110:03626:what writing techniques work best, which patterns of language most
p110:03627:successfully reach and hold readers. But like all tips they should be
p110:03628:considered carefully before being acted on.
p110:03629:   So don’t use the active voice “because it’s the right way.” Don’t write with
p110:03630:strong nouns and verbs “because you’re supposed to.” And don’t maintain
p110:03631:consistently good grammar “because only stupid people don’t.” Tips, not
p110:03632:laws. Think about these tips. Apply them generally. They will guide you to
p110:03633:successful writing.
p110:03634:   And do something else. Accept the fact that there is good writing and bad
p110:03635:writing. There is writing that runs, and there is writing that plods. There is
p110:03636:writing that wakes up readers and writing that puts them to sleep. So turn to
p110:03637:this book from time to time. Stretch your vocabulary. Read. A lot. And, most
p110:03638:important, develop your ear for the sound of written language. When you
p110:03639:have done these, you will have the knowledge and the wisdom to apply the
p110:03640:best tip of all: Use your own common sense.
